3 Syro-Malabar Liturgical Calendar ( Second Set) 
 General Instructions 
 1.   This calendar, “ Syro-Malabar Liturgical Calendar: Second 
 Set ”, is published for the liturgical celebration, based on the 
 decision of the Syro-Malabar Bishops’ Synod held in January 
 2021 and June 2023. 
 2.   According to the calendar of the East Syrian Church, the liturgical 
 year is divided into nine liturgical seasons. A brief explanation 
 of the spirit of the seasons is given at the beginning of every 
 season. 
 3.   The liturgical year is centered around the Holy Mysteries of the 
 Salvation History. Birth of Jesus ( Suvara-Yalda , Annunciation- 
 Nativity),   Baptism   ( Denha ,   Manifestation-Epiphany),   Passion 
 and Death of our Lord ( Sauma Ramba , Great Fast), Resurrection- 
 Easter ( Qyamtha ) Pentecost ( Šleehe ), Growth of the Church 
 ( Kaitha ), Transfiguration and Exaltation of the Cross and the 
 Second Coming of Jesus ( Ēliya-Sleeva-Mušae ), and Dedication 
 of the Church ( Qudaš Edtha ) are the major salvific events 
 commemorated. 
 4.   Feasts are divided into three groups based on their importance. 
 They are: Our Lord’s ( Maranaya ) Feasts, Important Feasts and 
 Ordinary Feasts. The readings are arranged accordingly. 
 a.   On our Lord’s Feasts and Commemorations the readings of 
 the feasts are given before the readings of the season. 
 b.   The Important Feasts are divided into two: Marian Feasts ( Eg . 
 Feast of the Immaculate Conception) and Commemorations 
 of martyrs and saints ( Eg .   Dukhrana   of St. Thomas,   etc .) 
 c.   The Ordinary Feasts are subdivided into two: i) of the 
 Oriental Tradition and ii) of the Universal Church. 
 Note : Those given in bold letters are more important in these groups. 
 5.   If the feast of   Denha   (Epiphany) falls on a Sunday, that Sunday is 
 considered to be the First Sunday of the season of   Denha . If the

 Twenty five day fast, Three day fast, Eight day fast and Fifteen 
 day fast.

 first Sunday of the Season of   Sleeva   (Cross). The readings for 
 the season of   Elia   are continued till that Sunday. If the feast of 
 Sleeva falls on a Sunday, it will be considered as the first Sunday 
 of   Sleeva   (Cross). 
 11.   Four readings are given for all Sundays and for all other 
 Important Feasts. 
 12.   The Table of Feasts in the Liturgical Year is given as Appendix II. 
 13.   Readings for Sacraments, Feasts and for other special occasions 
 are given as Appendix   II.

 17.   In the East Syrian Tradition, there are three   Qudašas   (Anaphoras): 
 The First   Qudaša   ( Qudaša   of Mar Addai and Mar Mari) is used 
 from the Monday after Palm Sunday till the end of the season 
 of Dedication of the Church; the Second   Qudaša   ( Qudaša   of 
 Mar Theodore) is used from the first Sunday of the season of 
 Annunciation till and including Palm Sunday; the Third   Qudaša 
 ( Qudaša   of Mar Nestorius) is used on five days,   i.e ., on the feast of 
 Denha   (Epiphany), The Friday of John the Baptist, the memory of 
 the Greek Fathers, the Wednesday of the three days fast, and on 
 Pesaha (Holy Thursday). Any of these three   Qudašas   can be used 
 on other occasions too.

6 Syro-Malabar Liturgical Calendar ( Second Set) 
 Syro-Malabar Liturgical Year 
 The liturgical year in our Church is so devised that the liturgical 
 community in different seasons and different places may closely reflect 
 on and experience the redemptive work of Jesus Christ ( Išo Mišiha ). The 
 Liturgical year celebrates the salvific mysteries of Jesus in its integrity 
 and fullness. Through fasting and abstinence and commemorations of 
 the saints, the liturgical year helps us to accomplish our destiny, the 
 heaven. 
 The whole liturgical year and the feasts in it are arranged in 
 order to focus the attention on the resurrection of Jesus, which is the 
 centre of the mysteries of Christ. Instead of limiting the memorial of 
 this great event, the early Church started extending this celebration 
 to every Sunday. Later, all salvific mysteries from the Incarnation to 
 the Second Coming of our Lord and the mysteries of the Church are 
 celebrated all throughout the year by dividing it into several seasons. 
 This arrangement of the liturgical year, which starts with the season of 
 Annunciation and ends in the season of the Dedication of the Church, 
 is an occasion for catechesis too. 
 Seasons of Syro-Malabar Liturgical Year: 
 Season of Annunciation ( Suvara ) 
 Season of Nativity ( Yalda ) 
 Season of Manifestation ( Denha ) 
 Season of the Great Fast ( Sauma Ramba ) 
 Season of Resurrection ( Qyamtha ) 
 Season of the Apostles ( Šleehe ) 
 Season of Summer ( Kaitha ) 
 Seasons of Elijah-Cross-Moses ( Ēliya-Sleeva-Mušae ) 
 Season of the Dedication of the Church ( Qudash Edtha )

 Annunciation-Nativity ( Suvara-Yalda ) 
 The liturgical year of the Syro-Malabar church begins with 
 the season of Annunciation that commemorates the mystery of 
 incarnation. This season is centred around the feast of Nativity of 
 the Lord on the 25th of December. There are four weeks before the 
 Christmas and two weeks after it. As a preparation for the celebration 
 of Nativity, the St. Thomas Christians observes 25 days of fasting from 
 1st to 25th December. 
 The saving plan that is accomplished in Jesus, begins with His 
 birth. The season of Annunciation is called suvara in Syriac, which 
 means ‘proclamation’, ‘announcement’ etc. The season of nativity is 
 called yalda in Syriac. 
 The announcement made to Mary by angel Gabriel was the Good 
 News to the humanity that was expecting the Saviour. Thus, these 
 liturgical seasons are formulated in the context of the Mystery of 
 Incarnation of Jesus that was fulfilled in the course of time. During 
 these days, the Church commemorates also the auspicious events of 
 the announcement of the birth of John the Baptist, the precursor and 
 his birth. 
 As God gave a son to Abraham in order to form a people who 
 believe in Him, He gives a son to Zachariah before the formulation 
 of a community of new covenant. It is through the individuals and 
 their progeny who lived a life of deep faith and who observed God’s 
 commandments that God’s plan had started, continued and to be 
 continued. The readings of first and third Sundays of this season gives 
 us this message. The prominent themes of the season of Nativity are 
 the holy birth of Jesus, the visit of the Magi, the flight in to Egypt, the 
 event of the presentation Jesus in the Temple and His infancy. 
 The birth of Jesus which is the centre of this season is the 
 completion of Old Testament expectations and prophesies. The 
 event of incarnation of Son-God, who came to wipe way the sin that 
 came through Eve, from holy Virgin Mary is the great Good News

 of this season. Man has the ‘Emmanuel’ experience in the event 
 of Incarnation of Christ. The Incarnation of Jesus leads man to the 
 experience of paradise. 
 In the two weeks that follow the feast of Nativity, all the events 
 that are connected to the Mystery of Incarnation of Jesus and the 
 episodes before His public life are proclaimed. The lesson that the 
 Magi gives us is to seek and find the Christ who makes present himself 
 in our daily life. The child Jesus who was in the Temple, teaches us that 
 the real worship is possible only when we are in a real relationship 
 with God. 
 The holy Church venerates Blessed Virgin Mary as the first person 
 who revealed Christ to the world during the season of Annunciation. 
 On the last Friday of this season, the Friday before the Epiphany, we 
 commemorate the feast of the Divine Motherhood of Mary, apart 
 from the regular remembrance of Virgin Mary, the Mother of God on 
 Wednesdays. This commemoration of Divine Motherhood of Mary on 
 the last Friday of this season is in relation with the commemoration 
 of saints, who revealed Christ to the world, on the Fridays of the 
 season of Epiphany. The scriptural readings, prayers and hymns of the 
 seasons of Annunciation and Nativity, remind us: as Old Testament 
 people who thirsted for the Saviour, realizing the pitiful condition of 
 self, the New Testament people should understand their helplessness 
 and sinful situations in order to walk towards Christ and thus to give 
 a space to Him in their hearts.

 SEASON OF EPIPHANY (DENHA) 
 The   Syriac   word   Denaha   has   several   meanings   like   dawn, 
 manifestation, epiphany and revelation. In the season of epiphany 
 we commemorate, the manifestation that began during the Baptism 
 of Jesus at river Jordan. Jesus reveals Himself to the world, and the 
 Father and the Holy Spirit give witness to it. “This is my beloved Son, 
 with whom I am well pleased” (Mt. 3:17). The Mystery of Holy Trinity 
 is revealed at the baptism of Jesus Christ. 
 The Lord’s Epiphany that is celebrated on 6th January, is called 
 “feast of   pindikuthi ” in the northern region of Kerala and “feast 
 of   rakkuli ” in the southern region. The expression, “the feast of 
 pindikuthi ” is derived from the practice of lighting wicks around the 
 stem of plantain tree, by making procession around it shouting   el 
 payya   (God is Light), to honour and praise Christ who is ‘the Light of 
 the world’. The other name, ‘ rakkuli ’ is derived from the practice of 
 taking bath (ritual bath) in the nearby river or pond, on the eve of this 
 feast, by our ancestors as reminder of the Baptism of Jesus Christ at 
 river Jordan. It was completely a religious rite. 
 All the readings of this season of Epiphany that commemorate the 
 public life of Christ, lead us to the personality of Christ. The election 
 of the apostles, as an important event of the public life, is also a

 subject matter of   meditation of the season of Epiphany. During this 
 season the Church teaches us that Christ, the Lamb of God who takes 
 away the sin, has come to find the sinners and, the salvation and the 
 forgiveness of sin are through Christ, the Son of God. Through the 
 readings of this season, we receive the knowledge and experience that 
 Jesus is the Fulfilment of the law, the Bread of life and the Merciful 
 and Good shepherd who send the apostles to the sheep who have no 
 shepherds. 
 On the Fridays of this season, the Church commemorates the 
 saints who have witnessed Jesus Christ and His redemptive Mysteries 
 through their lives. On the first Friday of this season, the Church 
 commemorates St. John the Baptist, who experienced the glory of 
 Jesus, even when he was in the maternal womb and who received the 
 testimony of the glory of revelation from heaven at the Baptism of 
 Jesus. On the following Fridays, the Church remembers the saints who 
 bore great witness to the life of Jesus Christ: Saint Peter and Saint 
 Paul, the Evangelists, Saint Stephan, the Greek and the Syriac Fathers 
 of the Church, the Patron saint of the parish. The last Friday of the 
 season of Epiphany is the Day of remembrance of All the Departed 
 Souls who are in the hope of Resurrection. 
 Since there should be seven weeks for the season of Great Fast 
 before the Feast of Easter, the number of the weeks in the season 
 of Epiphany may be differed. Another particularity of this season is 
 the observance of the Three Days Fast, the Rogation of Ninivites; the 
 Three Days Fast start eighteen days before the beginning of Great Fast 
 which is filled with the grace of repentance. 
 Together with the commemoration of Baptism of Jesus, this season 
 reminds each Christian to think deeply about his or her reception 
 of Baptism that made him/her the ‘son/daughter of God’ and the 
 responsibilities taken up through it. Thus, the season of Epiphany is 
 the time to make attempt to love Jesus passionately by knowing Him, 
 by living in Him and   by bearing witness to Him.

 SEASON OF THE GREAT FAST ( SAUMA RAMBA ) 
 The Passion, Death and Resurrection (The Paschal Mystery) that 
 culminates the salvation event, take place at the end of Jesus’ public 
 life. The seven weeks between the Season of Epiphany and the Feast of 
 Easter are set aside for prayer, penance and fasting. 
 The base for the season known as the Great Fast, is the ‘forty days 
 fast’ of Jesus. We normally call this season as ‘fifty days fast’. It is 
 known as ‘fifty days fast’ may be from the practice of the observance 
 of this fast from the Pethurtha Sunday to the Easter Sunday by the St. 
 Thomas Christians. The Syriac term pethurtha signifies ‘to put an end’ 
 and ‘turning back’. What is meant by this term is to indicate that the 
 days of sumptuous food and celebrations are over.

 It is on the Wednesday of fourth week that we commemorate the 
 half of the Great Fast. This Wednesday particularly reminds us to 
 find solutions for the drawbacks of our life, to get identified with the 
 Paschal Mystery of Jesus and to live more faithfully in the grace of 
 fasting. 
 This season teaches us that the craze for material wealth causes sin. 
 The wealth which is God’s gift is to be properly used and to be shared 
 with others. The salvation experience is received through repentance, 
 prayer, fasting and acts of charity. This season also gives us the inner 
 vision that we become the true disciples when we accept God as the 
 wealth, like the apostles who followed Jesus renouncing everything. 
 Salvation is received only when we confess sin. Those who throw 
 away the dirty mantle of sin can follow Christ. The Church teaches 
 that our sanctification is God’s will and our sanctification is possible 
 only through Jesus Christ who offered His life – the Body and Blood – 
 for us to the Father. 
 Our body tempts us to do the undesired evil, but to live without 
 sinning is a great suffering. The season of Great Fast demands us to 
 suffer for the sanctification of our whole life. The sufferings that we 
 face and the sacrifices we do help us to grow in faith and to get united 
 with Christ. The Church teaches us that only when we stay together 
 faithfully to the sufferings of Christ, we can enter into the joy and 
 peace of the Resurrection of Christ.

 SEASON OF RESURRECTION ( QYAMTHA ) 
 The season of Res urrection is the seven weeks between the Feast of 
 Resurrection (Easter) and Pentecost. It is the occasion to rejoice in the 
 new life that is received through the Resurrection of the Redeemer, 
 Jesus Christ. All the prayers and hymns of this season reflect this joy. 
 The first feast that is celebrated in the Church is the Easter which 
 is the center of Christian faith. From the Council of Nicea (325), this 
 feast is celebrated in the universal church, on the first Sunday after 
 the full moon that comes after 21 March, the equinox. In this season 
 the Church commemorates all the events from Easter to Pentecost in 
 seven weeks of time. The East Syrian Church celebrate on the first 
 Friday after Easter ‘the Feast of all the Saints’. The eighth day after 
 Easter, which is called ‘Sunday of Neophytes’, ‘New Sunday’, is the 
 day of profession of faith of St. Thomas, the Apostle in the presence 
 of Christ, the Resurrected: ‘My Lord and My God’. The first week of 
 the season of Resurrection has been set aside as the week of ‘new 
 Christians’ who received baptism newly. Thus, there was the tradition 
 of giving baptism along with the feast of Resurrection. In this season 
 the Church commemorates the promise of Christ to be with the Church 
 till the end of the world and the giving of the authority to Simon Peter, 
 other apostles and their successors to shepherd the Church. 
 On the 40th day after Easter, Church celebrates the Feast of the 
 Ascension of the Lord and meditate on the following themes: Eternal 
 life, Resurrection, Entering the heavenly Kingdom, Christ is the only 
 Redeemer through whom we can enter into the eternal life, Disciples 
 always prayed in the Temple, Invitation to wait with hope for the 
 Pentecostal experience in the house of the Lord where the heaven and 
 earth meet toge ther.

 SEASON OF THE APOSTLES ( ŠLEEHE ) 
 The seven weeks from the feast of Pentecost are known as the season 
 of the Apostles. During this season, Church invites our attention to 
 the gift of Holy Spirit, the gifts and fruits of the Holy Spirit. The feast 
 of Pentecost has an inevitable relation with the salvation history. In 
 the Old Testament we read that the Israelites had celebrated the feast 
 of Pentecost in connection with harvesting season. The term   pentecost 
 means fifty – feast on the fiftieth day. It was also the feast of offering 
 of first fruits in connection with harvesting. But later on, this feast 
 turned out to be the commemoration of Israelites becoming God’s 
 people by His covenant. 
 In the New Testament, a ‘new’ meaning was given to this feast. It 
 is on the fiftieth day after the Resurrection, the Holy Spirit descended 
 on the Apostles. It was on that day the ‘Church – Ecclesia’ was officially

 inaugurated. It is after the Pentecost that the apostles who were filled 
 with the Holy Spirit, went to the extremities of the world with the 
 message of the New Covenant and laid foundations to different church 
 communities. The Syriac term   sleeha   means ‘one who is sent’. This 
 liturgical season reminds us the fact that all those who have received 
 Baptism and Anointing are ‘apostles’, ‘who are sent’. 
 The first Friday of this season is called ‘Golden Friday’. The cure 
 that the paralytic received in the Name Jesus, is a guarantee that 
 whatever we ask in the Name of Jesus, will be granted. 
 It is the responsibility of God’s people to proclaim the message of 
 salvation to the entire world by being filled with the Holy Spirit. This 
 season inspires us to be missionaries like the apostles. The declaration 
 of St. Thomas, the Apostle, who is sent by Christ, ‘Let us go and die 
 with Him’ is an invitation to all of us who are believers. This season 
 reveals the God who protects those who are sent. 
 It reminds the responsibility to live a life, centred on Word of God, 
 by those who are sent to proclaim the Good News. It also emphasises 
 the necessity to bear witness fearlessly to God who is the Word. It is 
 when a grain of wheat falls to the ground and dies, it will produce 
 fruit. Therefore, Jesus reminds us to do the mission courageously and 
 that we gain eternal life through the suffering. The primary mission 
 of all the Christians is to give witness to Christ in any situations. 
 The Holy Spirit, the Helper sent by the Father, lives in all the 
 baptised individuals and showers His graces and fruits. The assistance 
 of the Holy Spirit is essential to remember all that is taught by Christ 
 and to obey His commandments in every moment of life. This season 
 reminds also that if we lose God’s Spirit by sinning, the consequence 
 will be utter destruction. 
 We shall partake in the communion and unity of the apostles who 
 established new ecclesial communities with the message of the Master 
 by travelling all over the world. Holding fast the truth that we are the 
 temple of the Holy Spirit and with His continuous assistance, let us 
 proclaim Christ as the Apostles did.

 SEASON OF SUMMER (KAITHA) 
 The liturgical Season of Summer starts by commemorating the 
 Twelve Apostles of our Lord. This season that lasts ordinarily for seven 
 weeks is called the Season of Summer (season of harvesting, fruits, 
 etc.) The term   khaitha’   means ‘summer’. Harvesting and the storing of 
 the fruits are normally done in the season of summer. The liturgical 
 season of   Khaitha   is a time that commemorates and celebrates the 
 growth of the Church as well as the harvesting of spiritual fruits. 
 Ordinarily, it has seven weeks. This season reminds us that the Church, 
 that is rooted and extended to the far corners of the world by the 
 missionary activities of the apostles and martyrs, started harvesting 
 the fruits of sanctity and martyrdom. 
 This season that connects the Seasons of Apostles and Elia-Sleeva 
 Moses reminds us the activities of the Church and the necessity of 
 being prepared to the second coming of the Saviour. 
 The first Sunday of this season is the Feast of Twelve Apostles. 
 This feast is also called   Noosardel   or ‘Feast of God’. On the Fridays of 
 this season, the saints and martyrs who have made to grow the Church, 
 watering with their own blood and witnessed Christ courageously, 
 are remembered. Thus, the Church remembers: on the first Friday, 
 St. Jacob of Nisibis who sacrificed his life for the Lord Christ; on the 
 second Friday Mar Mari who was the disciple of Mar Addai and the 
 founder of Persian Church; on the fifth Friday St. Shmoni and her 
 seven Sons (2 Mac 7); and on the sixth Sunday Patriarch St. Simon 
 Barsabe (AD 323-341) who is reported to be the author of   Lakumara 
 (the Hymn ‘Lord of all’) and his fellow martyrs. The Church invites the 
 faithful to imitate their exemplary life. 
 It is when we stay united to the Christ that the Church can grow 
 and yield fruits. The Lord patiently awaits for the fruits of goodness 
 from the children of the Church. The readings of this season give 
 warning about the tragedies if we do not produce good fruits. Even 
 when the forces of darkness attack the Church – the arc of salvation, 
 Christ, the bridegroom of the Church, will lead her to the heavenly 
 Jerusalem. Therefore, this season gives us the hope that those who

 SEASONS OF ELIJAH-CROSS-MOSES 
 ( Ēliya-Sleeva-Mušae ) 
 The season of Elijah-Cross-Moses indicates the victory of the 
 Cross and the second coming of the Lord. The feast of the Exaltation of 
 the Holy Cross celebrated on the 14th of September is the focal point 
 of this season. The first ecclesial community believed that before the 
 second coming of Christ, Elijah would come (Mal 4:5) and will argue 
 with the son of destruction and convince the world about his fault. 
 This belief was grown because of the reality that Elijah was present 
 with the Lord at the time of transfiguration. The Lord’s transfiguration 
 is the symbol of Lord’s second coming. At the time of transfiguration, 
 Moses also was with the Lord; that might be the reason that this 
 liturgical season developed centring around the Cross. 
 The Sunday after 14th September is considered as first Sunday 
 of Sleeva. Till then the readings of the season of Elijah is continued. 
 When the feast of the Exaltation of the Holy Cross falls on a Sunday, 
 that will be the first Sunday of Sleeva. 
 There was the belief in the first ecclesial community, what Jesus 
 told about the sign that would appear in the sky before the second 
 coming of Christ (Mt. 24:30), is the Cross. Therefore, the strength 
 and the victory of the Cross are commemorated in this season. The 
 prayers and hymns of this season include the apparition of Cross to 
 the emperor Constantine, consequent victory and the finding of the 
 Cross by the queen Helena, the mother of Constantine. Like Moses 
 who showed the way to the Israelites, extending his staff on the Red 
 sea, Christ saved the humanity by showing the way to the paradise 
 through the Cross. 
 During this season the Church invites us to prepare for the 
 second coming of the Lord by assimilating the prophets of the true 
 God and the commands and messages given through them by God, and 
 by producing the fruits of repentance and conversion. The mother 
 Church gives us the warning that you will perish unless you walk in 
 the way of repentance abstaining from all evils, in the conviction that 
 the desires of body are against the spirit.

 Since the day of the Lord is imminent, the Church invites 
 us to attain the eternal life like the prudent virgins, ministering 
 him vigilantly, and through attentiveness, faithfulness, prudence, 
 equanimity, diligent service and continuous prayer. 
 The bronze serpent that was raised by Moses in the Old 
 Testament, is symbol of Jesus who was raise on the Cross to share the 
 salvation. One who takes pride in the Name of the Lord, takes pride 
 in the Cross, the sign of salvation, and in the sufferings. This season 
 reminds us that all those who follow Him, who has died on the Cross 
 and ascended into the glory of resurrection, have to bear the Cross. 
 The readings of this season give us the conviction that it is inevitable 
 to go through the suffering and death in order to enter into the glory. 
 The manifestation of the glory of Christ, who has fulfilled God’s 
 will by accepting suffering and death, happened on the Cross. The 
 Lord who will appear as the Judge at the end of the world, is the 
 one who has seated at the right side of the Glory through His Death, 
 Resurrection and Ascension. This season inspires the faithful to suffer 
 with Christ who was raised by God, because He emptied himself even 
 unto death on the Cross, in humility, to enter into glory. 
 Death and final judgments are the themes of this Season of 
 Moses. Jesus gives the consolation words that ‘the one who endures to 
 the end, will be saved’. The Church teaches: bear fruits, enduring faith 
 is fruitful, confront sufferings with faith to receive consolation and 
 joy.

 Season of the Dedication of the Church ( Qudash Edtha ) 
 Qudash Edtha   means dedication of the Church. The season of the 
 Dedication of the Church consists of the last four weeks in the liturgical 
 year. These four weeks are arranged on the basis of the dedication of 
 the four holy places in the Old Testament (Ex. 40:1-17; Joshua 18:1; 1 
 Kings 8:62-66; Ezra 3:2). 
 This season indicates Church’s eschatological victory and her 
 entrance into the heavenly Jerusalem. The prayers, readings and 
 hymns are brimming with the beauty that how Christ makes the 
 Church His bride. 
 The season of the Dedication of the Church commemorates the 
 face to face meeting of the Church as bride with her bridegroom Jesus 
 Christ and her glorious entrance to eternal joy. The tabernacle in the 
 Old Testament is a symbol of the Church. The Israelites experienced 
 God’s presence in this man-made tabernacle. 
 Those who find God in Christ and those who are vigilant enough 
 as the wise and prudent virgins, will enter with Jesus into the eternal 
 tabernacle, the heavenly bridal chamber, which is not made by the 
 human hands. Christ, the bridegroom, leads the Church, His bride, not 
 to a temporary tabernacle but to the eternal heavenly bridal chamber. 
 The Church exhorts her faithful to foster the awareness of “we 
 have no lasting city here” and to live their life in view of heaven, 
 where their names are written, and its everlasting joy. She invites the 
 faithful to participate in the heavenly worship in truth and spirit by 
 giving the insight that the Lord and the Lamb is the true temple in 
 heaven. 
 The liturgical year is organized in such a way that the People 
 of God complete their pilgrimage in this world which they started 
 by participating in the Mystery of Incarnation, complete with the 
 participation in the heavenly glory of Christ.

 Appendix I 
 Feasts in the Syro-Malabar Liturgical Year 
 There are two types of liturgical calendars in the Christian 
 Churches:   Temporal Cycle   and   Sanctoral Cycle . The Temporal Cycle 
 is centered around the salvific events fulfilled in Jesus Christ; and 
 this aims at guiding the community of the faithful into a profound 
 meditation of the redemptive mysteries through the spirit of the 
 Liturgical Seasons and thereby encouraging them to lead a true 
 Christian life. The Sanctoral Cycle is organized according to the feast 
 of saints; and in it the liturgical calendar follows the rhythm of the 
 Fixed Feasts and Movable Feasts. East Syriac calendar, which the 
 Syro-Malabar Church follows, is based on the temporal cycle; still, we 
 can find elements of the Sanctoral cycle in the liturgical year of this 
 Church. 
 The Second Vatican Council in consonance with the Tradition, 
 exhorts to give more importance to the feasts of the Lord than the 
 feasts of the saints. Therefore, feasts of the saints are not celebrated 
 on Sundays, which is the day of Our Lord. 
 The list of the Feasts is arranged according to the spirit of the 
 liturgical seasons and important feasts can be recognized easily. 
 110 feasts are included in this calendar. They are divided into three 
 groups, Our Lord’s   (Maranaya)   Feasts, Important Feasts and Ordinary 
 Feasts. The Ordinary Feasts are subdivided into two: a. of the Oriental 
 Tradition b. of the universal Church. 
 Eastern Churches have the tradition of integrating the feasts of 
 saints in the Temporal Cycle. Fridays are considered important next to 
 Sundays. Therefore, the feasts of the saints who became victorious by 
 witnessing the Paschal Mysteries are celebrated either individually or 
 in groups on Fridays. Their feasts are mostly celebrated on the Fridays 
 in the seasons of   Denha ,   Šleehe   and   Kaitha   which commemorate the 
 public ministry of Jesus, activities of the Apostles, and the growth of 
 the Church respectively. Some feasts that should be observed to ensure 
 the identity and Tradition of the Church are celebrated on some other 

--- PAGE 88 ---

89 
 Fridays. In order to link the passion, death and resurrection of our 
 Lord, the day of All the Departed is celebrated on the last Friday of 
 Denha   and the All Saints’ day is celebrated on the first Friday after the 
 feast of Resurrection. 
 The feasts of Holy   Qurbana   (Eucharist) and Holy Trinity are 
 listed in the Feasts of our Lord. The   Pesaha   is the ideal day for 
 the feast of the Holy   Qurbana . On the Feast of   Denha   and in the Season 
 of   Denha,   the Holy Trinity is specially remembered.   However, since 
 the faithful live amidst other rites and Churches, and many Religious 
 Congregations work among our community, some of the feasts of the 
 Latin Church have been given importance in our Church too. Feast of 
 the Holy of Blessed Sacrament, Feast of Sacred Heart, Feast of Christ 
 the King, and Feast of Our Lady of Mount Carmel are some among 
 them and they are included in the calendar. The feast of the Sacred 
 Heart of Jesus helps to meditate on the Mercy and Love of God. Since in 
 some areas, the feast of All Saints and of All the Departed are observed 
 according to the calendar of the Latin Church, they are also included 
 in this calendar. Feasts of local importance can be celebrated locally. 
 Each individual Church has a serious responsibility to keep its 
 identity and spirit. This calendar reflects an effort to be faithful to 
 this sacred responsibility. The feasts of our Lord ( Maranaya ), feasts of 
 Blessed Virgin Mary and some feasts of the saints are given in bold 
 letters, according to their importance. Those in small letters are 
 ordinary feasts and they have only two readings in the   Qurbana .
