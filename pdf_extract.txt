3 Syro-Malabar Liturgical Calendar ( Second Set)  General Instructions  1.   This calendar, “ Syro-Malabar Liturgical Calendar: Second  Set ”, is published for the liturgical celebration, based on the  decision of the Syro-Malabar Bishops’ Synod held in January  2021 and June 2023.  2.   According to the calendar of the East Syrian Church, the liturgical  year is divided into nine liturgical seasons. A brief explanation  of the spirit of the seasons is given at the beginning of every  season.  3.   The liturgical year is centered around the Holy Mysteries of the  Salvation History. Birth of Jesus ( Suvara-Yalda , Annunciation-  Nativity),   Baptism   ( Denha ,   Manifestation-Epiphany),   Passion  and Death of our Lord ( Sauma Ramba , Great Fast), Resurrection-  Easter ( Qyamtha ) Pentecost ( Šleehe ), Growth of the Church  ( Kaitha ), Transfiguration and Exaltation of the Cross and the  Second Coming of Jesus ( Ēliya-Sleeva-Mušae ), and Dedication  of the Church ( Qudaš Edtha ) are the major salvific events  commemorated.  4.   Feasts are divided into three groups based on their importance.  They are: Our Lord’s ( Maranaya ) Feasts, Important Feasts and  Ordinary Feasts. The readings are arranged accordingly.  a.   On our Lord’s Feasts and Commemorations the readings of  the feasts are given before the readings of the season.  b.   The Important Feasts are divided into two: Marian Feasts ( Eg .  Feast of the Immaculate Conception) and Commemorations  of martyrs and saints ( Eg .   Dukhrana   of St. Thomas,   etc .)  c.   The Ordinary Feasts are subdivided into two: i) of the  Oriental Tradition and ii) of the Universal Church.  Note : Those given in bold letters are more important in these groups.  5. 6 Syro-Malabar Liturgical Calendar ( Second Set)  Syro-Malabar Liturgical Year  The liturgical year in our Church is so devised that the liturgical  community in different seasons and different places may closely reflect  on and experience the redemptive work of Jesus Christ ( Išo Mišiha ). The  Liturgical year celebrates the salvific mysteries of Jesus in its integrity  and fullness. Through fasting and abstinence and commemorations of  the saints, the liturgical year helps us to accomplish our destiny, the  heaven.  The whole liturgical year and the feasts in it are arranged in  order to focus the attention on the resurrection of Jesus, which is the  centre of the mysteries of Christ. Instead of limiting the memorial of  this great event, the early Church started extending this celebration  to every Sunday. Later, all salvific mysteries from the Incarnation to  the Second Coming of our Lord and the mysteries of the Church are  celebrated all throughout the year by dividing it into several seasons.  This arrangement of the liturgical year, which starts with the season of  Annunciation and ends in the season of the Dedication of the Church,  is an occasion for catechesis too.  Seasons of Syro-Malabar Liturgical Year:  Season of Annunciation ( Suvara )  Season of Nativity ( Yalda )  Season of Manifestation ( Denha )  Season of the Great Fast ( Sauma Ramba )  Season of Resurrection ( Qyamtha )  Season of the Apostles ( Šleehe )  Season of Summer ( Kaitha )  Seasons of Elijah-Cross-Moses ( Ēliya-Sleeva-Mušae )  Season of the Dedication of the Church ( Qudash Edtha )

7 Syro-Malabar Liturgical Calendar ( Second Set)  Annunciation-Nativity ( Suvara-Yalda )  The liturgical year of the Syro-Malabar church begins with  the season of Annunciation that commemorates the mystery of  incarnation. This season is centred around the feast of Nativity of  the Lord on the 25th of December. There are four weeks before the  Christmas and two weeks after it. As a preparation for the celebration  of Nativity, the St. Thomas Christians observes 25 days of fasting from  1st to 25th December.  The saving plan that is accomplished in Jesus, begins with His  birth. The season of Annunciation is called suvara in Syriac, which  means ‘proclamation’, ‘announcement’ etc. The season of nativity is  called yalda in Syriac.  The announcement made to Mary by angel Gabriel was the Good  News to the humanity that was expecting the Saviour. Thus, these  liturgical seasons are formulated in the context of the Mystery of  Incarnation of Jesus that was fulfilled in the course of time. During  these days, the Church commemorates also the auspicious events of  the announcement of the birth of John the Baptist, the precursor and  his birth.  As God gave a son to Abraham in order to form a people who  believe in Him, He gives a son to Zachariah before the formulation  of a community of new covenant. It is through the individuals and  their progeny who lived a life of deep faith and who observed God’s  commandments that God’s plan had started, continued and to be  continued. The readings of first and third Sundays of this season gives  us this message. The prominent themes of the season of Nativity are  the holy birth of Jesus, the visit of the Magi, the flight in to Egypt, the  event of the presentation Jesus in the Temple and His infancy.  The birth of Jesus which is the centre of this season is the  completion of Old Testament expectations and prophesies. The  event of incarnation of Son-God, who came to wipe way the sin that  came through Eve, from holy Virgin Mary is the great Good News

The Incarnation of Jesus leads man to the  experience of paradise.  In the two weeks that follow the feast of Nativity, all the events  that are connected to the Mystery of Incarnation of Jesus and the  episodes before His public life are proclaimed. The lesson that the  Magi gives us is to seek and find the Christ who makes present himself  in our daily life. The child Jesus who was in the Temple, teaches us that  the real worship is possible only when we are in a real relationship  with God.  The holy Church venerates Blessed Virgin Mary as the first person  who revealed Christ to the world during the season of Annunciation.  On the last Friday of this season, the Friday before the Epiphany, we  commemorate the feast of the Divine Motherhood of Mary, apart  from the regular remembrance of Virgin Mary, the Mother of God on  Wednesdays. This commemoration of Divine Motherhood of Mary on  the last Friday of this season is in relation with the commemoration  of saints, who revealed Christ to the world, on the Fridays of the  season of Epiphany. The scriptural readings, prayers and hymns of the  seasons of Annunciation and Nativity, remind us: as Old Testament  people who thirsted for the Saviour, realizing the pitiful condition of  self, the New Testament people should understand their helplessness  and sinful situations in order to walk towards Christ and thus to give  a space to Him in their hearts.

In the season of epiphany  we commemorate, the manifestation that began during the Baptism  of Jesus at river Jordan. Jesus reveals Himself to the world, and the  Father and the Holy Spirit give witness to it. “This is my beloved Son,  with whom I am well pleased” (Mt. 3:17). The Mystery of Holy Trinity  is revealed at the baptism of Jesus Christ.  The Lord’s Epiphany that is celebrated on 6th January, is called  “feast of   pindikuthi ” in the northern region of Kerala and “feast  of   rakkuli ” in the southern region. The expression, “the feast of  pindikuthi ” is derived from the practice of lighting wicks around the  stem of plantain tree, by making procession around it shouting   el  payya   (God is Light), to honour and praise Christ who is ‘the Light of  the world’. The other name, ‘ rakkuli ’ is derived from the practice of  taking bath (ritual bath) in the nearby river or pond, on the eve of this  feast, by our ancestors as reminder of the Baptism of Jesus Christ at  river Jordan. It was completely a religious rite.  All the readings of this season of Epiphany that commemorate the  public life of Christ, lead us to the personality of Christ. During this  season the Church teaches us that Christ, the Lamb of God who takes  away the sin, has come to find the sinners and, the salvation and the  forgiveness of sin are through Christ, the Son of God. Through the  readings of this season, we receive the knowledge and experience that  Jesus is the Fulfilment of the law, the Bread of life and the Merciful  and Good shepherd who send the apostles to the sheep who have no  shepherds.  On the Fridays of this season, the Church commemorates the  saints who have witnessed Jesus Christ and His redemptive Mysteries  through their lives. On the first Friday of this season, the Church  commemorates St. John the Baptist, who experienced the glory of  Jesus, even when he was in the maternal womb and who received the  testimony of the glory of revelation from heaven at the Baptism of  Jesus. On the following Fridays, the Church remembers the saints who  bore great witness to the life of Jesus Christ: Saint Peter and Saint  Paul, the Evangelists, Saint Stephan, the Greek and the Syriac Fathers  of the Church, the Patron saint of the parish. The last Friday of the  season of Epiphany is the Day of remembrance of All the Departed  Souls who are in the hope of Resurrection.  Since there should be seven weeks for the season of Great Fast  before the Feast of Easter, the number of the weeks in the season  of Epiphany may be differed. Another particularity of this season is  the observance of the Three Days Fast, the Rogation of Ninivites; the  Three Days Fast start eighteen days before the beginning of Great Fast  which is filled with the grace of repentance.  Together with the commemoration of Baptism of Jesus, this season  reminds each Christian to think deeply about his or her reception  of Baptism that made him/her the ‘son/daughter of God’ and the  responsibilities taken up through it. 26 Syro-Malabar Liturgical Calendar ( Second Set)  It is on the Wednesday of fourth week that we commemorate the  half of the Great Fast. This Wednesday particularly reminds us to  find solutions for the drawbacks of our life, to get identified with the  Paschal Mystery of Jesus and to live more faithfully in the grace of  fasting.  This season teaches us that the craze for material wealth causes sin.  The wealth which is God’s gift is to be properly used and to be shared  with others. The salvation experience is received through repentance,  prayer, fasting and acts of charity. This season also gives us the inner  vision that we become the true disciples when we accept God as the  wealth, like the apostles who followed Jesus renouncing everything.  Salvation is received only when we confess sin. Those who throw  away the dirty mantle of sin can follow Christ. The Church teaches  that our sanctification is God’s will and our sanctification is possible  only through Jesus Christ who offered His life – the Body and Blood –  for us to the Father.  Our body tempts us to do the undesired evil, but to live without  sinning is a great suffering. The season of Great Fast demands us to  suffer for the sanctification of our whole life. The sufferings that we  face and the sacrifices we do help us to grow in faith and to get united  with Christ.
